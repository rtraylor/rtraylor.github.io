Abstract

Editor's note: This paper represents the second installment of a masters thesis by Jonathan Johnson. This paper continues the development of the theory of summation chains of sequences. The concept of sum-related is defined: two sequences are sum-related if one sequence appears in the summation chain of the other. The main result is a theorem to determine if two given sequences are sum-related.

Introduction

Editor's note: Previously, the concept of a summation chain of sequences was defined. We reproduce the example and introduction here: Given a complex-valued sequence [latex](a_n)^{\infty}_{n=1}[/latex], the sequence of partial sums of [latex](a_n)[/latex] is given by the sequence [latex, display=true](a_1,a_1+a_2,a_1+a_2+a_3,\ldots,\sum^n_{i=1}a_i,\ldots).[/latex] The sequence of differences of [latex](a_n)[/latex] is given by the sequence [latex, display=true](a_1, a_2-a_1,a_3-a_2,\ldots,a_n-a_{n-1},\ldots).[/latex] The processes of finding the sequence of partial sums and finding the sequence of differences of a sequence are inverses of each other so every sequence is the sequence of differences of its sequence of partial sums and the sequence of partial sums of its sequence of differences. Every sequence has a unique sequence of partial sums and a unique sequence of differences so it is always possible to find the sequence of partial sums of the sequence of partial sums and repeat the process ad infinitum. Similarly, we can find the sequence of differences of the sequence of differences and repeat ad infinitum. The result is a doubly infinite sequence or "chain'' of sequences where each sequence is the sequence of partial sums of the previous sequence and the sequence of differences of the following sequence. Example Let [latex]a^{(0)}[/latex] be the sequence defined by [latex]a^{(0)}_n=(-1)^{n+1}[/latex] for all [latex]n\in\mathbb{N}[/latex]. For all integers [latex]m>0[/latex], let [latex]a^{(m)}[/latex] be the sequence of partial sums of [latex]a^{(m-1)}[/latex], and for all integers [latex]m<0[/latex], let [latex]a^{(m)}[/latex] be the sequence of differences of [latex]a^{(m+1)}[/latex]. [latex, display = true]\begin{array}{r|ccccccccc}\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\a^{(2)} & 1 & 1 & 2 & 2 & 3 & 3 & 4 & 4 & \cdots\\a^{(1)} & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots\\a^{(0)} & 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 & \cdots\\a^{(-1)} & 1 & -2 & 2 & -2 & 2 & -2 & 2 & -2 & \cdots\\a^{(-2)} & 1 & -3 & 4 & -4 & 4 & -4 & 4 & -4 & \cdots\\\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots\\\end{array}[/latex] Every sequence can be used to create a unique chain of sequences. Editor's note: This installment examines the relationship between sequences and their summation chains, and provides a way to determine whether two sequences are sum-related. [/nextpage]

Summation Chains and Sequences

Sum-Related Chains

Consider the following sequences with their [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chains. [/nextpage] [nextpage title="..."]   Definition. Let M be a [latex]\mathbb{Z}[/latex]-Module. Let [latex](a_n)[/latex] and [latex](b_n)[/latex] be sequences in M. [latex](a_n)[/latex] is sum-related to [latex](b_n)[/latex], denoted [latex](a_n)\sim_{\Sigma}(b_n)[/latex], if [latex](a_n)[/latex] is a sequence in [latex]\Phi_{\Sigma} ((b_n))[/latex]. If two sequences are sum-related, then one sequence can be obtained by finding the sequences of partial sums of the sequences of partial sums of the other sequence. Remark. The trivial sequence [latex](0,0,0,\ldots)[/latex] is sum-related only to itself.

Proposition.

Sum relation defines an equivalence class.

Proposition.

Let c and d be [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chains. If there exists [latex]m\in \mathbb{Z}[/latex] such that [latex]c(m,n)=d(m,n)[/latex], for all [latex]n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex], then c=d.

Proposition.

Let M be a [latex]\mathbb{Z}[/latex]-Module. Let c be a [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain in M. Let [latex]d:\mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{N}\to M[/latex] be defined such that there exists [latex]k\in \mathbb{Z}[/latex] such that [latex]d(m,n)=c(m+k,n)[/latex] for every [latex]m\in \mathbb{Z}[/latex] and [latex]n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex], thend is a [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain,and every sequence in c is sum-related to every sequence in d. Definition. Let c be a [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain, and let [latex]k\in \mathbb{Z}[/latex].The [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain [latex]c^{\,k}[/latex] is defined for all integers m and natural numbers n, by [latex]c^{\,k}(m,n)=c(m+k,n)[/latex]. [latex]c^{\,k}[/latex] is said to be the [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain c shifted by k. Remark. If [latex]s_1[/latex] and [latex]s_2[/latex] are sequences generating [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chains [latex]c_1[/latex] and [latex]c_2[/latex] respectively, then saying [latex]s_1 \sim_{\Sigma} s_2[/latex] is equivalent to saying [latex]c_1=c_2^k[/latex] for some integer k. When do two sequences appear in the same summation chain? By the above definition, this is the same as asking when sequences are sum-related. Consider the sequence [latex](x_k)=(0,0,1,-1,1,-1,\ldots)[/latex]. The sequences sum-related to [latex](x_k)[/latex] are the sequences that are in [latex]\Phi_{\Sigma[\mathbb{R}]}((x_k))[/latex]. [latex, display = true]\Phi_{\Sigma[\mathbb{R}]}((x_{k}) : \begin{array}{c|ccccccc} \color{SteelBlue}{m} \backslash \color{LightSeaGreen}{n} & \color{LightSeaGreen}{1} &\color{LightSeaGreen}{2} & \color{LightSeaGreen}{3} & \color{LightSeaGreen}{4} &\color{LightSeaGreen}{5} &\color{LightSeaGreen}{6} &\color{LightSeaGreen}{\cdots}\\\color{SteelBlue}\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\\\color{SteelBlue}{2} & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 2 & 2 & \cdots\\\color{SteelBlue}{1} & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots\\\color{SteelBlue}{0} & 0 & 0 & 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 & \cdots\\\color{SteelBlue}{-1} & 0 & 0 & 1 & -2 & 2 & -2 & \cdots\\\color{SteelBlue}{-2} & 0 & 0 & 1 & -3 & 4 & -4 & \cdots\\\color{SteelBlue}{\vdots} & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\\\end{array}[/latex] Notice that each sequence in the chain starts with two zeros followed by a one. The sequences [latex](0,1,10,20,30,\ldots)[/latex] and [latex](0,0,4,5,6,\ldots)[/latex] could not be sum-related to [latex](x_k)[/latex] since they do not begin with this sequence. This strategy can eliminate most pairs of sequences.

Lemma.

Given two nontrivial sequences [latex](a_k)[/latex] and [latex](b_k)[/latex] such that [latex](a_k)\sim_{\Sigma}(b_k)[/latex], define [latex]N_a[/latex] and [latex]N_b[/latex] to be the indices of the first nonzero terms of [latex](a_k)[/latex] and [latex](b_k)[/latex] respectively, then [latex]N_a= N_b[/latex], and [latex]a_{N_a}=b_{N_b}[/latex]. In general, after exploiting the above lemma, it is difficult to determine sum relation of sequences. It is not possible to exhaustively check every sequence in a chain. However, when every nonzero element in the [latex]\mathbb{Z}[/latex]-Module has infinite order, the corollary from the previous post tells where to look for sum-related sequences. Thus in this case, a closed form procedure for determining sum relation can be used. [/nextpage] [nextpage title="..."]

Theorem.

Let M be a [latex]\mathbb{Z}[/latex]-Module. such that the order of every nonzero element in M is infinite. Let [latex](a_n)[/latex] and [latex](b_n)[/latex] be sequences in M, and let [latex]c_a=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_n))[/latex]. Let [latex]N_1[/latex] be the index of the first nonzero term of [latex](a_n)[/latex], and let [latex]N_2[/latex] be the index of the first nonzero term of [latex](b_{n})[/latex]. Thus, [latex](a_{n})\sim_{\Sigma}(b_{n})[/latex] if and only if the following conditions hold.
  1. [latex]N_1=N_2[/latex], if this is true let [latex]N=N_1=N_2[/latex]
  2. [latex]a_N=b_N[/latex],
  3. There exists [latex]m \in \mathbb{Z}[/latex] such that [latex]m\cdot a_N=b_{N+1}-a_{N+1}[/latex],
  4. [latex]b_n=c_a(m,n), \forall n \in \mathbb{N}[/latex].
Remark.  When the integer m exists in the theorem above, it is unique. Example. Let [latex]M=\mathbb{Z}[/latex]. Let [latex](a_n)=(1,-1,-1,1,0,0,0,0,0,\ldots)[/latex] and [latex]b_n=n^2[/latex], [latex]\forall n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex]. Let [latex]c_a=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_n))[/latex]. The index of the first nonzero terms of both sequences is 1. Also, [latex]a_1=b_1=1[/latex] so (1) and (2) are satisfied. [latex]b_2-a_2=5=5\cdot a_1[/latex] so (3) holds. If there is a chain relation, [latex](b_n)[/latex] will be the fifth sequence in [latex]c_a[/latex]. (4) has already been shown for [latex]n=1[/latex] and [latex]n=2[/latex]. Using formulas from the theorem, the computation for the other indices is as follows: [latex, display = true]c_a(5,3)={6 \choose 4}(1)+{5 \choose 4}(-1)+{4 \choose 4}(-1)=15-5-1=9=b_3[/latex] For [latex]n\geq 4[/latex], [latex, display=true]\begin{aligned}c_a(5,n)=&\sum^{n}_{k=1}{4+n-k \choose 4}a_k\\=&{n+3 \choose 4}(1)+{n+2 \choose 4}(-1)+{n+1 \choose 4}(-1)+{n \choose 4}(1)\\=&\frac{(n+3)(n+2)(n+1)n}{24}-\frac{(n+2)(n+1)n(n-1)}{24}\\&-\frac{(n+1)n(n-1)(n-2)}{24}+\frac{n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)}{24}\\=&\, n^2\\=&\, b_n\\\end{aligned}[/latex] Therefore, by the theorem above, [latex](a_n)\sim_{\Sigma}(b_n)[/latex]. Example Let [latex]M=\mathbb{R}[/latex]. Let [latex]a_n=\frac{1}{n}[/latex] and [latex]b_n=n[/latex], [latex]\forall n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex]. Let [latex]c_a=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_n))[/latex]. The index of the first nonzero terms of both sequences is 1. Also, [latex]a_1=b_1=1[/latex] so (1) and (2) are satisfied. However [latex]b_2-a_2=1.5[/latex] which cannot be obtained by multiplying an integer by [latex]a_1[/latex]. Therefore, [latex](a_n)\nsim_{\Sigma}(b_n)[/latex]. Example Let [latex]M=\mathbb{Z}[/latex]. Let [latex]a_n=n^2-3n+2[/latex] and [latex]b_n=-\frac{n^4+3n^3+14n^2-48n+32}{7}[/latex], [latex]\forall n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex]. Let [latex]c_a=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_n))[/latex]. The index of the first nonzero terms of both sequences is 3. Also, [latex]a_3=b_3=2[/latex] so (1) and (2) are satisfied. [latex]b_4-a_4=-6=-3\cdot a_3[/latex] so (3) holds. If there is a chain relation, [latex](b_n)[/latex] will be the third sequence down in [latex]c_a[/latex]. However, [latex]c_a(-3,5)=0[/latex] while [latex]b_5=-\frac{108}{7}[/latex]. Therefore, [latex](a_n)\nsim_{\Sigma}(b_n)[/latex]. Example Let [latex]M=\mathcal{Z}[X][/latex]. Let [latex]a_n=\sum^{n-1}_{i=0}(i+1)x^{n-i}[/latex], [latex]\forall n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex], and let [latex]b_1=x[/latex] and [latex]b_n=x^n-x^{n-1}[/latex], [latex]n\geq 2[/latex]. Let [latex]c_a=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_n))[/latex]. The index of the first nonzero terms of both sequences is 1. Also, [latex]a_1=b_1=x[/latex] so (1) and (2) are satisfied. [latex]b_2-a_2=(x^2-x)-(x^2+2x)=-3x=-3\cdot a_1[/latex] so (3) holds. If there is a chain relation, [latex](b_n)[/latex] will be the third sequence down in [latex]c_a[/latex]. (4) has already been shown for [latex]n=1[/latex] and [latex]n=2[/latex]. [latex, display=true]\begin{aligned}c_a(-3,3)&={3 \choose 1}(x)-{3 \choose 2}(x^2+2x)+{3 \choose 3}(x^3+2x^2+3x)\\&=3x+3(x^2+2x)+x^3+2x^2+3x\\&=x^3-x^2\\&=b_3\\\end{aligned}[/latex]   For [latex]n\geq 4[/latex], [latex, display=true]\begin{aligned}c_a(-3,n)=&\sum^{3}_{k=0}(-1)^{3-k}{3 \choose 3-k}a_{n-3+k}\\=&-{3 \choose 0}\sum^{n-4}_{i=0}(i+1)x^{n-i-3}+{3 \choose 1}\sum^{n-3}_{i=0}(i+1)x^{n-i-1}\\&-{3 \choose 2}\sum^{n-2}_{i=0}(i+1)x^{n-i-1}+{3 \choose 3}\sum^{n-1}_{i=0}(i+1)x^{n-i}\\=&-\sum^{n-1}_{i=3}(i-2)x^{n-i}+3\sum^{n-1}_{i=2}(i-1)x^{n-i}-3\sum^{n-1}_{i=1}ix^{n-i}\\ &\quad+\sum^{n-1}_{i=0}(i+1)x^{n-i}\\=&-\sum^{n-1}_{i=3}(i-2)x^{n-i}+3\sum^{n-1}_{i=3}(i-1)x^{n-i}-3\sum^{n-1}_{i=3}ix^{n-i}\\&+\sum^{n-1}_{i=3}(i+1)x^{n-i}+3x^{n-2}-3x^{n-1}-6x^{n-2}+x^n+2x^{n-1}+3x^{n-2}\\=&\sum^{n-1}_{i=3}[-(i-2)+3(i-1)-3i(i+1)]x^{n-i}+x^n-x^{n-1}\\=&\, x^n-x^{n-1}\\=&\, b_n\\\end{aligned}[/latex] Therefore,[latex](a_n)\sim_{\Sigma}(b_n)[/latex]. Chains of Sequences with Leading Zeros

Adding zeros to the beginning of a sequence adds columns of zeros to be beginning of the sequences summation chain.

Lemma.

Let c be a nontrivial [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain in [latex]M[/latex]. Let [latex]N=\min\{n \in \mathbb{N}:c(0,n)\neq 0\}[/latex]. For every integer, m, and for every natural number, [latex]n<N[/latex], [latex]c(m,n)=0[/latex].

Proposition.

Let M be a [latex]\mathbb{Z}[/latex]-Module. Let c be a nontrivial [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain in [latex]M[/latex]. Let the natural number [latex]N=\min\{n \in \mathbb{N}:c(0,n)\neq 0\}[/latex]. Let [latex]d:\mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{N}\to M[/latex] be defined such that [latex]d(m,n) = c(m,n+N+1)[/latex] for every [latex]m\in \mathbb{Z}[/latex] and [latex]n\in \mathbb{N}[/latex]. Then d is a [latex]\Sigma[/latex]-chain.

Corollary

Let M be a [latex]\mathbb{Z}[/latex]-Module, and let [latex](a_n)[/latex] and [latex](b_n)[/latex] be sequences in M, such that for a positive integer k, [latex]b_{n+k}=a_n[/latex] for all natural numbers n and [latex]b_n=0[/latex] when [latex]n\leq k[/latex]. Let [latex]c_a=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_n))[/latex] and [latex]c_b=\Phi_{\Sigma}((a_b))[/latex]. For every integer m and natural number [latex]n[/latex], [latex]c_b(m,n+k)=c_a(m,n)[/latex], and [latex]c_b(m,n)=0[/latex] when [latex]n< k[/latex].

Conclusion

Editor's note: This paper represents the second installment of the concept of summation chains of complex valued sequences. Here, a new way to determine the relationship between two sequences was defined. The concept of sum-relation was defined, and a theorem provided a way to determine the relationship between two sequences.  Part 3 will discuss sequence chains generated by linear function rules.

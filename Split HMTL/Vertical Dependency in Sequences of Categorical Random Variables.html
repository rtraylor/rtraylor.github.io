Abstract

This paper develops a more general theory of sequences of dependent categorical random variables, extending the works of Korzeniowski (2013) and Traylor (2017) that studied first-kind dependency in sequences of Bernoulli and categorical random variables, respectively. A more natural form of dependency, sequential dependency, is defined and shown to retain the property of identically distributed but dependent elements in the sequence. The cross-covariance of sequentially dependent categorical random variables is proven to decrease exponentially in the dependency coefficient [latex]\delta[/latex] as the distance between the variables in the sequence increases. We then generalize the notion of vertical dependency to describe the relationship between a categorical random variable in a sequence and its predecessors, and define a class of generating functions for such dependency structures. The main result of the paper is that any sequence of dependent categorical random variables generated from a function in the class [latex]\mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] that is dependency continuous yields identically distributed but dependent random variables. Finally, a graphical interpretation is given and several examples from the generalized vertical dependency class are illustrated.

Introduction

Many statistical tools and distributions rely on the independence of a sequence or set of random variables. The sum of independent Bernoulli random variables yields a binomial random variable. More generally, the sum of independent categorical random variables yields a multinomial random variable. Independent Bernoulli trials also form the basis of the geometric and negative binomial distributions, though the focus is on the number of failures before the first (or rth success). [2] In data science, linear regression relies on independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) error terms, just to name a few examples. The necessity of independence filters throughout statistics and data science, although real data rarely is actually independent. Transformations of data to reduce multicollinearity (such as principal component analysis) are commonly used before applying predictive models that require assumptions of independence. This paper aims to continue building a formal foundation of dependency among sequences of random variables in order to extend these into generalized distributions that do not rely on mutual independence in order to better model the complex nature of real data. We build on the works of Korzeniowski [1] and Traylor [3] who both studied first-kind (FK) dependence for Bernoulli and categorical random variables, respectively, in order to define a general class of functions that generate dependent sequences of categorical random variables. The next section gives a brief review of the original work by Korzeniowski [1] and Traylor [3]. In Section 2, a new dependency structure, sequential dependency is introduced, and the cross-covariance matrix for two sequentially dependent categorical random variables is derived. Sequentially dependent categorical random variables are identically distributed but dependent. Section 3.1 generalized the notion of vertical dependency structures into a class that encapsulates both the first-kind (FK) dependence of Korzeniowski [1] and Traylor [3] and shows that all such sequences of dependent random variables are identically distributed. We also provide a graphical interpretation and illustrations of several examples of vertical dependency structures. Probability Mass Flow for FK Dependent Bernoulli Random Variables[/caption] We repeat a section from A Generalized Multinomial Distribution from Dependent Categorical Random Variables  in order to give a review of the original first-kind (FK) dependency created by Korzeniowski. For the full details, see the previous work on the topic. Korzeniowski defined the notion of dependence in a way we will refer to here as dependence of the first kind (FK dependence). Suppose [latex](\epsilon_{1},...,\epsilon_{N})[/latex] is a sequence of Bernoulli random variables, and [latex]P(\epsilon_{1} = 1) = p[/latex]. Then, for [latex]\epsilon_{i}, i \geq 2[/latex], we weight the probability of each binary outcome toward the outcome of [latex]\epsilon_{1}[/latex], adjusting the probabilities of the remaining outcomes accordingly. Formally, let [latex]0 \leq \delta \leq 1[/latex], and [latex]q = 1-p[/latex]. Then define the following quantities [latex, display=true]\begin{aligned}p^{+} := P(\epsilon_{i} = 1 | \epsilon_{1} = 1) = p + \delta q &\qquad p^{-} :=P(\epsilon_{i} = 0 | \epsilon_{1} = 1) = q -\delta q\\q^{+} := P(\epsilon_{i} = 1 | \epsilon_{1} = 0) = p-\delta p&\qquad q^{-} := P(\epsilon_{i} = 0 | \epsilon_{1} = 0) = q + \delta p\end{aligned}[/latex] Given the outcome [latex]i[/latex] of [latex]\epsilon_{1}[/latex], the probability of outcome [latex]i[/latex] occurring in the subsequent Bernoulli variables [latex]\epsilon_{2}, \epsilon_{3},..., \epsilon_{n}[/latex] is [latex]p^{+}, i = 1[/latex] or [latex]q^{+}, i=0[/latex]. The probability of the opposite outcome is then decreased to [latex]q^{-}[/latex] and [latex]p^{-}[/latex], respectively. The figure above illustrates the possible outcomes of a sequence of such dependent Bernoulli variables. Korzeniowski showed that, despite this conditional dependency, [latex]P(\epsilon_{i} = 1) = p \quad\forall i[/latex]. That is, the sequence of Bernoulli variables is identically distributed, with correlation shown to be [latex, display = true]\text{Cor}(\epsilon_{i}, \epsilon_{j}) = \left\{\begin{array}{lr}\delta, & i=1 \\\delta^{2}, &i \neq j, \quad i,j \geq 2\end{array}\right.[/latex] These identically distributed but correlated Bernoulli random variables yield a Generalized Binomial distribution with a similar form to the standard binomial distribution. In the previous work, the concept of Bernoulli FK dependence was extended to categorical random variables. That is, given a sequence of categorical random variables with [latex]K[/latex] categories, [latex]P(\epsilon_{1} = i) = p_{i}[/latex], [latex]i = 1,\ldots,K,[/latex] [latex, display=true]\begin{aligned}P(\epsilon_{j} = i | \epsilon_{1} = i) &= p_{i}^{+} = p_{i} + \delta(1-p_{i});\\P(\epsilon_{j} = k | \epsilon_{1} = i) &= p_{k}^{-} = p_{k}-\delta p_{k}, i \neq k, \:\:k = 1,\ldots,K.\end{aligned}[/latex] Traylor proved that FK dependent categorical random variables remained identically distributed, and showed that the cross-covariance matrix of categorical random variables has the same structure as the correlation between FK dependent Bernoulli random variables. In addition, the concept of a generalized binomial distribution was extended to a generalized multinomial distribution. In the next section, we will explore a different type of dependency structure, sequential dependency. [/nextpage] [nextpage title="..."]

Sequentially Dependent Random Variables

[caption id="attachment_505" align="aligncenter" width="725"] Figure 2. Probability Mass Flow of Sequentially Dependent Categorical Random Variables, K=3.[/caption] [caption id="attachment_504" align="aligncenter" width="718"] Figure 3. Probability Mass Flow of FK Dependent Categorical Random Variables, K=3.[/caption] While FK dependence yielded some interesting results, a more realistic type of dependence is sequential dependence, where the outcome of a categorical random variable depends with coefficient δ on the outcome of the variable immediately preceeding it in the sequence. Put formally, if we let [latex]\mathcal{F}_{n} = \{\epsilon_{1},\ldots,\epsilon_{n-1}\}[/latex], then [latex]P(\epsilon_{n}|\epsilon_{1},\ldots,\epsilon_{n-1}) = P(\epsilon_{n}|\epsilon_{n-1}) \neq P(\epsilon_{n})[/latex]. That is, [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] only has direct dependence on the previous variable [latex]\epsilon_{n-1}[/latex]. We keep the same weighting as for FK-dependence. That is, [latex, display = true]\begin{array}{lr}P(\epsilon_{n} = j | \epsilon_{n-1} = j) = p_{j}^{+} = p_{j} + \delta(1-p_{j}),&\\ P(\epsilon_{n} = j | \epsilon_{n-1} = i) = p_{j}^{-} = p_{j}-\delta p_{j}, &\:\: j = 1,\ldots,K;\:\:i \neq j\end{array}[/latex] As a comparison, for FK dependence, [latex]P(\epsilon_{n}|\epsilon_{1},\ldots,\epsilon_{n-1}) = P(\epsilon_{n}|\epsilon_{1}) \neq P(\epsilon_{n})[/latex]. That is, [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] only has direct dependence on [latex]\epsilon_{1}[/latex], and [latex, display = true]\begin{array}{lr}P(\epsilon_{n} = j | \epsilon_{1} = j) = p_{j}^{+} = p_{j} + \delta(1-p_{j}),&\\ P(\epsilon_{n} = j | \epsilon_{1} = i) = p_{j}^{-} = p_{j}-\delta p_{j},&\:\:j = 1,\ldots,K;\:\: i \neq j\end{array}[/latex] Let [latex]\epsilon = (\epsilon_{1},\ldots,\epsilon_{n})[/latex] be a sequence of categorical random variables of length n (either independent or dependent) where the number of categories for all [latex]\epsilon_{i}[/latex] is K. Denote [latex]\Omega_{n}^{K}[/latex] as the sample space of this random sequence. For example, [latex, display = true]\Omega_{3}^{3} = \{(1,1,1), (1,1,2), (1,1,3), (1,2,1), (1,2,2),\ldots,(3,3,1),(3,3,2),(3,3,3)\}[/latex] Dependency structures like FK-dependence and sequential dependence change the probability of a sequence [latex]\epsilon[/latex] of length n taking a particular [latex]\omega = (\omega_{1},\ldots,\omega_{n}) \in \Omega_{n}^{K}[/latex]. The probability of a particular [latex]\omega \in \Omega_{n}^{K}[/latex] is given by the dependency structure. For example, if the variables are independent, [latex]P((1,2,1)) = p_{1}^{2}p_{2}[/latex]. Under FK-dependence, [latex]P((1,2,1)) = p_{1}p_{2}^{-}p_{1}^{+}[/latex], and under sequential dependence, [latex]P((1,2,1)) = p_{1}p_{2}^{-}p_{1}^{-}[/latex]. See Figures 2 and 3 for a comparison of the probability mass flows of sequential dependence and FK dependence. Sequentially dependent sequences of categorical random variables remain identically distributed but dependent, just like FK-dependent sequences. That is,
Lemma 1 Let [latex]\epsilon = (\epsilon_{1},\ldots,\epsilon_{n})[/latex] be a sequentially dependent categorical sequence of length n with K categories. Then [latex, display=true]P(\epsilon_{j} = i) = p_{i}; \qquad i = 1,\ldots,K;\:\:j = 1,\ldots,n;\:\: n \in \mathbb{N}[/latex]

Cross-Covariance Matrix

The [latex]K \times K[/latex] cross-covariance matrix [latex]\Lambda^{m,n}[/latex] of [latex]\epsilon_{m}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] in a sequentially dependent categorical sequence has entries [latex]\Lambda_{i,j}^{m,n}[/latex] that give the cross-covariance [latex]\text{Cov}([\epsilon_{m} = i][/latex], [latex][\epsilon_{n} = j])[/latex], where [latex][\cdot][/latex] denotes an Iverson bracket. In the FK-dependent case, the entries of [latex]\Lambda^{m,n}[/latex] are given by [latex, display=true]\Lambda^{1,n}_{ij}=\left\{\begin{array}{lc}\delta p_{i}(1-p_{i}), & i = j \\-\delta p_{i}p_{j}, & i \neq j,\end{array}\right.\quad\,n \geq 2,[/latex] and [latex, display=true]\Lambda^{m,n}_{ij}=\left\{\begin{array}{lc}\delta^{2}p_{i}(1-p_{i}), & i = j \\-\delta^{2}p_{i}p_{j}, & i \neq j,\end{array}\right. \quad\,n > m; m\neq 1[/latex] Thus, the cross covariance between any two [latex]\epsilon_{m}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] in the FK-dependent case is never smaller than δ 2 times the independent cross-covariance. In the sequentially dependent case, the cross-covariances of [latex]\epsilon_{m}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] decrease in powers of δ as the distance between the two variables in the sequence increases.
Theorem 1 (Cross-Covariance of Dependent Categorical Random Variables). Denote [latex]\Lambda^{m,n}[/latex] as the [latex]K \times K[/latex] cross-covariance matrix of [latex]\epsilon_{m}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] in a sequentially dependent sequence of categorical random variables of length N, [latex]m \leq n[/latex], and [latex]n \leq N[/latex], defined as [latex]\Lambda^{m,n} = E[(\epsilon_{m} - E[\epsilon_{m}])(\epsilon_{n} - E[\epsilon_{n}])][/latex]. Then the entries of the matrix are given by [latex, display=true]\Lambda^{m,n}_{ij} = \left\{\begin{array}{lc}\delta^{n-m}p_{i}(1-p_{i}), & i = j \\-\delta^{n-m} p_{i}p_{j}, & i \neq j.\end{array}\right.[/latex]
The pairwise covariance between two Bernoulli variables in a sequentially dependent sequence is given in the following corollary
Corollary 1 Denote [latex]P(\epsilon_{i} = 1) = p[/latex]; [latex]i = 1,\ldots,n[/latex], and let [latex]q=1-p[/latex]. Under sequential dependence, [latex, display=true]\text{Cov}(\epsilon_{m}, \epsilon_{n}) = pq\delta^{n-m}.[/latex]
We give some examples to illustrate.   Example 1 (Bernoulli Random Variables). If we want to find the covariance between [latex]\epsilon_{2}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{3}[/latex], then we note that the set [latex]S = \{\omega \in \Omega_{3}^{2}\,:\,\omega_{2} = 1, \omega_{3} = 1\}[/latex] is given by [latex]S = \{(1,1,1),(0,1,1)\}[/latex]. [latex]P(\epsilon_{2} = 1, \epsilon_{3} = 1) = P(S)[/latex]. Thus, [latex, display=true]\begin{array}{rl}\text{Cov}(\epsilon_{2},\epsilon_{3})&=P(\epsilon_{2} = 1, \epsilon_{3} = 1)-P(\epsilon_{2} = 1)P(\epsilon_{3} = 1)\\&=pp^{+}p^{+} + qp^{-}p^{+}-p^{2}\\&= p^{+}(pp^{+} + qp^{-})-p^{2}\\&= p(p^{+}-p)\\&= pq\delta\end{array}[/latex] Similarly, [latex, display=true]\begin{array}{rl}\text{Cov}(\epsilon_{1}, \epsilon_{3}) &= P(\epsilon_{1} = 1, \epsilon_{3} = 1)-P(\epsilon_{1} = 1)P(\epsilon_{3} = 1)\\&= (pp^{+}p^{+} + pq^{-}p^{-})-p^{2} \\&= p((p+\delta q)^{2} + pq(1-\delta)^{2})-p^{2}\\&= p(p^{2} + pq+\delta^{2}q^{2} + \delta^{2}pq)-p^{2}\\&= p(p(p+q) + \delta^{2}q(q+p)- p^{2} \\&= p(p + \delta^{2}q)-p^{2}\\&= pq\delta^{2}\end{array}[/latex]   Example 2 (Categorical Random Variables). Suppose we have a sequence of categorical random variables, where K = 3. Then [latex][\epsilon_{m} = i][/latex] is the Bernoulli random variable with the binary outcome of 1 if [latex]\epsilon_{m} = i[/latex] and 0 if not. Thus, [latex, display=true]\text{Cov}([\epsilon_{m} = i], [\epsilon_{n} = j]) = P(\epsilon_{m} = i \vee \epsilon_{n}= j)-P(\epsilon_{m} = i)P(\epsilon_{n} = j).[/latex] We have shown that every [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] in the sequence is identically distributed, so [latex]P(\epsilon_{m} = i) = p_{i}[/latex] and [latex]P(\epsilon_{n} = j) = p_{j}[/latex]. [latex, display=true]\begin{array}{rl}\text{Cov}([\epsilon_{2} = 1], [\epsilon_{3} = 1]) &=(p_{1}p_{1}^{+}p_{1}^{+} + p_{2}p_{1}^{-}p_{1}^{+} + p_{3}p_{1}^{-}p{1}^{+})-p_{1}^{2} \\&= p_{1}^{+}(p_{1}p_{1}^{+} + p_{2}p_{1}^{-} + p_{3}p_{1}^{-})-p_{1}^{2} \\&= p_{1}^{+}p_{1}-p_{1}^{2} \\&=\delta p_{1}(1-p_{1})\end{array}[/latex] [latex, display=true]\begin{array}{rl}\text{Cov}([\epsilon_{2} = 1], [\epsilon_{3} = 2]) &=(p_{1}p_{1}^{+}p_{2}^{-} + p_{2}p_{1}^{-}p_{2}^{-}+p_{3}p_{1}^{-}p_{2}^{-})-p_{1}p_{2} \\&= p_{2}^{-}(p_{1}p_{1}^{+} + p_{2}p_{1}^{-} + p_{3}p_{1}^{-})-p_{1}p_{2}\\&= p_{1}p_{2}^{-}-p_{1}p_{2} \\&=-\delta p_{1}p_{2}\end{array}[/latex] We may obtain the other entries of the matrix in a similar fashion. So, the cross-covariance matrix for a [latex]\epsilon_{2}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{3}[/latex] with K = 3 categories is given by [latex, display=true]\Lambda^{2,3}=\delta\begin{pmatrix}p_{1}(1-p_{1})&-p_{1}p_{2}&-p_{1}p_{3}\\-p_{1}p_{2} & p_{2}(1-p_{2}) &-p_{2}p_{3}\\-p_{1}p_{3} &-p_{2}p_{3} & p_{3}(1-p_{3})\end{pmatrix}[/latex] Note that if [latex]\epsilon_{2}[/latex] and [latex]\epsilon_{3}[/latex] are independent, then the cross-covariance matrix is all zeros, because δ = 0.

Dependency Generators

Both FK-dependency and sequential dependency structures are particular examples of a class of vertical dependency structures. We denote the dependency of subsequent categorical random variables on a previous variable in the sequence as a vertical dependency. In this section, we define a class of functions that generate vertical dependency structures with the property that all the variables in the sequence are identically distributed but dependent.

Vertical Dependency Generators Produce Identically Distributed Sequences

Define the set of functions [latex, display=true]\mathscr{C}_{\delta} = \{\alpha: \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2} \to \mathbb{N} : \alpha(n) \geq 1 \wedge \alpha(n) < n\:\: \forall\:n\}.[/latex] The mapping defines a direct dependency of n on [latex]\alpha(n)[/latex], denoted [latex]n \stackrel{\delta}{\rightsquigarrow} \alpha(n)[/latex]. We have already defined the notion of direct dependency, but we formalize it here.
Definition. (Direct Dependency) Let [latex]\epsilon_{m}[/latex], [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] be two categorical random variables in a sequence, where [latex]m < n[/latex]. We say [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] has a direct dependency on [latex]\epsilon_{m}[/latex], denoted [latex]\epsilon_{n} \rightsquigarrow_{\delta} \epsilon_{m}[/latex], if [latex]P(\epsilon_{n}|\epsilon_{n-1},\ldots,\epsilon_{1}) = P(\epsilon_{n}|\epsilon_{m})[/latex].
Example 3. For FK-dependence, [latex]\alpha(n) \equiv 1[/latex]. That is, for any [latex]n, \epsilon_{n} \rightsquigarrow_{\delta} \epsilon_{1}[/latex] Example 4.  The function [latex]\alpha(n) = n-1[/latex] generates the sequential dependency structure. We now define the notion of dependency continuity.  
Definition. (Dependency Continuity) A function [latex]\alpha: \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2} \to \mathbb{N}[/latex] is dependency continuous if [latex]\forall n \exists j \in \{1,...,n-1\}[/latex] such that [latex]\alpha(n) = j[/latex]
  We require that the functions in [latex]\mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] be dependency continuous. Thus, the formal definition of the class of dependency generators [latex]\mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] is  
Definition. (Dependency Generator) We define the set of functions [latex]\mathscr{C}_{\delta} = \{\alpha : \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2} \to \mathbb{N}\}[/latex] such that
  •  [latex]\alpha(n) < n[/latex]
  • [latex]\forall n \exists j \in \{1,...,n-1\} : \alpha(n) = j[/latex]
and refer to this class as dependency generators.
Each function in [latex]\mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] generates a unique dependency structure for sequences of dependent categorical random variables where the individual elements of the sequence remain identically distributed.  
Theorem 2. (Identical Distribution of Sequences Generated by Dependency Generators) Let [latex]\alpha \in \mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex]. Then for any [latex]n\in \mathbb{N}, n \geq 2[/latex], the dependent categorical random sequence generated by [latex]\alpha[/latex] has identically distributed elements.

Graphical Interpretation and Illustrations

We may visualize the dependency structures generated by [latex]\alpha \in \mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] via directed dependency graphs. Each [latex]\epsilon_{n}[/latex] represents a vertex in the graph, and a directed edge connects [latex]n[/latex] to [latex]\alpha(n)[/latex] to represent the direct dependency generated by [latex]\alpha[/latex]. We can see that a path exists from any [latex]n[/latex] back to 1. This  is a visual way to see the result of Theorem2,  in that if a path exists from any [latex]n[/latex] back to 1, then the variables in that path must be identically distributed. It is neither increasing nor decreasing. The previous examples have all been nondecreasing.   A Prime Example Let [latex]\alpha \in \mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] be defined in the following way. Let [latex]p_{m}[/latex] be the [latex]m[/latex]th prime number, and let [latex]\{kp_{m}\}[/latex] be the set of all postive integer multiples of [latex]p_{m}[/latex]. Then the set [latex]\mathscr{P}_{m} = \{kp_{m}\} \setminus \cup_{i=1}^{m-1}\{kp_{i}\}[/latex] gives a disjoint partitioning of [latex]\mathbb{N}[/latex]. That is, [latex]\mathbb{N} = \sqcup_{m}\mathscr{P}_{m}[/latex], and thus for any [latex]n \in \mathbb{N}, n \in \mathscr{P}_{m}[/latex] for exactly one [latex]m[/latex]. Now let [latex]\alpha(n) = m[/latex]. Thus, the function is well-defined. We may write [latex]\alpha: \mathbb{N}_{\geq 2} \to \mathbb{N}[/latex] as [latex]\alpha[\mathscr{P}_{m}] = \{m\}[/latex]. As an illustration, [latex, display=true]\begin{aligned}\alpha[\{2k\}] &= \{1\} \\\alpha[\{3k\}\setminus \{2k\}] &= \{2\} \\\alpha[\{5k\}\setminus (\{2k\}\cup \{3k\})] &= \{3\} \\&\qquad\vdots\\\alpha[\{kp_{m}\}\setminus (\cup_{i=1}^{m-1}\{kp_{i}\})] &= \{m\}\end{aligned}[/latex] [/nextpage] [nextpage title="..."]

Conclusion

This paper has extended the concept of dependent random sequences first put forth in the works of Korzeniowski [1] and Traylor [3] and developed a generalized class of vertical dependency structures. The sequential dependency structure was studied extensively, and a formula for the cross-covariance obtained. The class of dependency generators was defined and shown to always produce a unique dependency structure for any [latex]\alpha \in \mathscr{C}_{\delta}[/latex] in which the a sequence of categorical random variables under that [latex]\alpha[/latex] is identically distributed but dependent. We provided a graphical interpretation of this class, and illustrated with several key examples.
